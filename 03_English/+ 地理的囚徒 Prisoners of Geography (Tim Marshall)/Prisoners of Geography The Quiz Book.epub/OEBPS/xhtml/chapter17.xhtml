8 a) The Svalbard Islands

Russia and Norway dispute ownership of the islands. Most countries and international organizations recognize the islands as being under (limited) Norwegian sovereignty. The numerous competing territorial claims between the Arctic countries tend to stem from the same issues – the need to safeguard routes for military and commercial shipping, the desire to own the natural riches of the region, and the fear that other countries could gain an advantage.

11 The discovery of energy deposits in the form of gas and oil

As the ice melts, it has revealed the potential existence of vast quantities of undiscovered natural gas and oil reserves. In 2008 the US Geological Survey estimated that there could be 1,669 trillion cubic feet of natural gas, 44 billion barrels of natural gas liquids and 90 billion barrels of oil in the region.

12 b) 1905

The great Norwegian explorer Roald Amundsen charted his way across in a small ship with just six other crew in an expedition that lasted from 1903 to 1906. He passed King William Island, went through the Bering Strait and into the Pacific. He knew he’d made it when he spotted a whaling ship from San Francisco coming from the other direction. In his diary on 26 August 1905 he wrote: ‘The Northwest Passage was done. My boyhood dream – at that moment it was accomplished. A strange feeling welled up in my throat; I was somewhat over-strained and worn – it was weakness in me – but I felt tears in my eyes.’

13 c) 2014

The cargo ship was the Nunavik, carrying 23,000 tonnes of nickel ore from Canada to China. The polar route is 40% shorter and with deeper waters than the Panama Canal. This allows ships to carry more cargo and cut their journey time, saving tens of thousands of dollars in fuel costs and reducing greenhouse gas emissions. As the ice in the Arctic recedes, the sea lanes through the region are more accessible for longer periods of the year. By 2040 the route is expected to be open for up to two months each year, transforming trade links across the ‘High North’ and causing knock-on effects as far away as Egypt and Panama in terms of the revenues they enjoy from the Suez and Panama canals.

33 The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea

One of the tenets of the convention is that a signatory has exclusive economic rights from its shore to a limit of 200 nautical miles (unless this conflicts with another country’s limits), and can declare it an Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ). The oil and gas in the zone is therefore considered to belong to the state. Depending on the continental shelf, a country can apply to extend its control up to 350 nautical miles from its coast. Given that the Arctic is largely sea, and the amount of competing claims and emerging resources in the area, UNCLOS becomes particularly relevant. Russia, for example, argues that the underwater Lomonosov Ridge is an extension of Siberia’s continental shelf, but this is problematic for the other Arctic countries as it runs all the way to the North Pole.

35 b) 1820

Who discovered Antarctica is a matter of debate. At around the same time in January of that year, both Thaddeus von Bellingshausen of the Russian Empire and British Royal Navy officer Edward Bransfield reported sighting land. Although Captain James Cook had reached the Antarctic circle almost fifty years earlier, his expedition never actually sighted land.
