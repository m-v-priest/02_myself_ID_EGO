2 The Treaty of Tordesillas

The treaty resolved the disputes between Spain and Portugal over the first sightings made by their explorers, drawing a line north to south that lay west of the Cape Verde islands. Any lands to the west of the line would belong to Spain, and to the east would be Portugal’s.

Latin America was first encountered by Europeans during the ‘Age of Discovery’, a period between the fifteenth and seventeenth centuries when European empires, including the British, Spanish, Portuguese and Dutch, began to explore and claim the region as their own. But of course there was already a huge native population, estimated as anywhere between 50 and 100 million, when they arrived. Over the following three centuries it is estimated that somewhere between 75 and 90% of that population died from violence, disease, displacement and famine.

6 c) James Monroe

The Monroe Doctrine in 1823 was intended to establish the New World and Old World as completely separate entities – the USA would stay out of Europe and Europe would stay out of the Americas. Although the USA wasn’t necessarily in a position to enforce this in the nineteenth century, and there were instances where it was broken, by the twentieth century US military strength had grown, and it became a central part of US foreign policy.

Spanish is the official language in most of Latin America, apart from Belize, Brazil, Guyana, French Guiana and Suriname.

Brazil is the country with the greatest number of Portuguese speakers in the world. It is the only country in Latin America where Portuguese is the official language, although it is also a minority language in Argentina, Paraguay, Uruguay and Venezuela.

Quechua is an indigenous language family that originated in Peru. It is the most widespread of the languages that predate European colonization; it is also spoken in Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, Colombia, Ecuador and Peru.

21 a) They set out on an expensive naval arms race

First Chile and then Argentina vied to unseat Brazil as the region’s naval power. All three spent heavily throughout the late nineteenth century, culminating in the expensive purchase of battleships known as dreadnoughts in the first decades of the twentieth century.

22 c) The populated rim

South America is in effect a demographically hollow continent, with the majority of the population living on the coastline. This is a geographical problem introduced by European settlers who stayed near the coasts, concentrating on getting the wealth out of each region and on to foreign markets. Thus most of the continent’s biggest cities were established near the coasts, and all roads from the interior were developed to connect to the capitals but not to each other. Even today many South American countries haven’t invested in developing the interior of their lands.

26 b) The Great Escarpment

33 Angel Falls, in Venezuela

It is 979 metres high and is 150 metres wide at the base. The waterfall was named for James Angel, an American adventurer who crash-landed his plane nearby in 1937.

34 Rio de Janeiro

Until 1960, the capital was Rio de Janeiro, at which point, in order to further develop the interior of the country, it became Brasilia, almost 1,000 km northwest, at the centre of the country.

35 The Falkland Islands

The Falklands archipelago lies 650 km east of Patagonia and is a British overseas territory. Argentina disputes the UK’s claim to the islands, and in April 1982 ordered an invasion. A month later a British task force arrived to reclaim the territory and Argentina surrendered on 14 June. However, it hasn’t abandoned its claim; it is an offence in Argentina to produce a map that describes the islands as anything other than the ‘Islas Malvinas’.

39 b) Cape Horn

Before the Panama Canal was built, Cape Horn was the only sea route between the Atlantic and Pacific oceans. As well as being a much longer distance to travel, Cape Horn was a perilous place for shipping, with huge waves, high winds and icebergs to navigate. Over the centuries hundreds of ships have been lost, and thousands of lives.

40 a) MERCOSUR

MERCOSUR, or the Southern Common Market, is an economic and political bloc promoting trade between member countries. Venezuela joined in 2012, but was suspended in 2016, and Bolivia became a member in 2024. UNASUR is the Union of South American Nations, sometimes also referred to as the South American Union originally containing 12 South American countries but by 2019 most members had left, and it is now largely inactive. BRICS contains Brazil, Russia, India, China and South Africa. OAS, the Organization of American States, includes all countries of the Americas.
