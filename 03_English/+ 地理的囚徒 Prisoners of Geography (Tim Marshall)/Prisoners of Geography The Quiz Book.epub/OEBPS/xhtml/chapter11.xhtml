2 a) The Hermit Kingdom

Korea had attempted to isolate itself following centuries of being a target for domination, occupation and plunder, or occasionally simply a route on the way to somewhere else. The Mongols came and went, as did the Chinese Qing dynasty and the Japanese several times. So for a while the country preferred not to engage with the outside world, cutting many of its trade links in the hope that it would be left alone.

3 The Gobi Desert

The Gobi Desert is the fifth largest desert in the world, at about 1.3 million km2, and provides a natural defence. Nomadic warriors from ancient times might have been able to attack south across it, but a modern army would be spotted massing there weeks before it was ready to advance, and it would need incredibly long supply lines running across inhospitable terrain before it got into Inner Mongolia (part of China) and close to the heartland. There are few roads fit to move heavy armour, and few habitable areas. The Gobi Desert is a massive early-warning system cum defensive line.

4 a) The Yellow River

It earned the nickname because the Yellow River basin is subject to frequent and devastating floods. But the river is to China what the Nile is to Egypt – the cradle of its civilization, where its people learned to farm, and to make paper and gunpowder.

5 c) A third

In 2022, about 37 million people out of a total population of about 125 million. The vast majority – over 90% – of Japan’s population is urban. Hundreds of Japan’s rural towns and villages are suffering depopulation.

6 a) It was lacking in natural materials such as coal, oil and metal ores

Japan had few of the natural resources required to become an industrialized nation. It had limited and poor-quality supplies of coal, very little oil, scant quantities of natural gas, limited supplies of rubber and a shortage of many metals. This is as true now as it was a hundred years ago, although offshore gas fields are being explored along with undersea deposits of precious metals. Nevertheless, in 2022 it was the world’s largest importer of natural gas, and fifth-largest importer of oil.

7 c) Water

Although Tibet contains all of these resources, it is water that is so important to China. Three of China’s great rivers, the Yellow, Yangtze and Mekong, have their source in the Tibetan Plateau, which is why Tibet is known as ‘China’s water tower’. To China, a country with a population of 1.4 billion, ensuring it has control of its water source is of key importance.

8 DMZ (Demilitarized Zone)

The defeat of Japan in the Second World War in 1945 left Korea divided along the 38th parallel. North of it was a communist regime overseen first by the Soviets and later by communist China; south of the line was a pro-American dictatorship called the Republic of Korea (ROK).

9 True

In June 1950, North Korea crossed the 38th parallel, intent on reuniting the peninsula under one communist government. In September 1950 the USA, leading a United Nations force, surged into Korea, pushing the northern troops back and up almost to the Yalu River and the border with China. Chinese troops poured across the Yalu and nearly three years of fierce fighting ensued with massive casualties on all sides before they ground to a halt along the current border and agreed a truce, but not a treaty.

10 Seoul

South Korea’s capital, the mega-city of Seoul, lies just 50 km or so south of the 38th parallel and the DMZ. Almost half of South Korea’s 51 million people live in the greater Seoul region, which is home to much of its industry and financial centres, and it is all within range of North Korean artillery. Its position makes it vulnerable to surprise attacks from its neighbour, whose capital is much further away and partially protected by mountainous terrain.

14 Durand Line

The code uses the first letter of each word.

The Afghanistan–Pakistan border is named after Sir Henry Mortimer Durand, the foreign secretary of the colonial government of India. However, in 1949 the Afghan government ‘annulled’ the agreement, believing it to be an artificial relic of the colonial era. Since then Pakistan has tried to persuade Afghanistan to change its mind, Afghanistan refuses, and the Pashtuns on each side of the mountains try to carry on as they have for centuries by ignoring the border and maintaining their ancient connections.

15 The Grand Canal in China

First started in the fourth century BCE, the Grand Canal took centuries to complete. It provides an important route between the south and north of the country and links the Yellow River to the Yangtze.

19 East Pakistan

When the British left the Indian Subcontinent in 1947, the region was divided into India and Pakistan. But the latter was split between two non-contiguous areas – East Pakistan and West Pakistan, separated by some 1,500 km of Indian territory – with little to hold the nation together. In 1971 East Pakistan rebelled and seceded.

20 Kashmir

The issue of Kashmir is a long-running one that has led to several outbreaks of war and prevents India and Pakistan from forming more cordial relations. Strategically it is an important area. Full control would give India a window into Central Asia and a border with Afghanistan, while denying Pakistan a border with China. Vice versa if Pakistan had control, plus it would have greater water security as the Indus River passes through the Indian-controlled part of Kashmir before entering Pakistan and running the full length of the country.

21 b) India and Pakistan

The two countries have had various wars and skirmishes over disputed territory, in 1947, 1965, 1984, 1987 and 1999. Tensions continue to this day, and both countries have nuclear weapons.

24 b) The Himalayas

War did break out between the two sides, from October to November 1962, due to rising tensions over the border and China’s invasion of Tibet. But despite not always having the warmest relationship, the two countries have largely avoided direct armed conflict as they are effectively walled off from each other over land by the highest mountain range in the world. It is practically impossible to advance large military columns through or over the Himalayas to invade one another’s territory.

25 The Belt and Road Initiative

This is the collective term for China’s planned infrastructure projects, unveiled in 2013, to create routes across land and sea, connecting China with various cities and ports around the world.

26 a) A dagger pointed at the heart of Japan

Japan feared Chinese or Russian control in Korea, from where it would be possible to launch an attack on Japan. Both the First Sino-Japanese War (1894–5) and the Russo-Japanese War (1904–5) were fought to thwart Chinese and Russian influence in Korea. Controlling the peninsula removed the threat.

27 The Nine Dash Line

The line marks the territory in the South China Sea that China claims as its own, sometimes shown with ten dashes to include Taiwan. However, many of its claims over the more than 200 tiny islands and reefs in this region clash with those of its neighbours, including Malaysia, Taiwan, Vietnam, the Philippines and Brunei. China wants to control the passageways through the islands in this area, and thus access to the world’s most important shipping lanes. In wartime these routes could easily be cut off, thus blockading China. It’s also thought that there are energy resources beneath the South China Sea.

42 Fiery Cross Reef

To further its claims in the South China Sea, China is using dredging and land-reclamation methods to begin turning a series of reefs and atolls into islands. Fiery Cross Reef, in the Spratly Islands, is now an island complete with a port and a runway that could host fighter jets, giving China far more control of the skies over the region than it currently has. Another reef has had artillery units stationed on it.
