2 The Sykes–Picot Agreement

In 1916, when the Ottoman Empire began to collapse, the British diplomat Sir Mark Sykes and his French counterpart, François Georges-Picot, agreed that north of the line was to be under French control, south of it under British hegemony. The term ‘Sykes–Picot’ arbitrarily has become shorthand for the various decisions made in the first third of the twentieth century that betrayed promises given to tribal leaders and that partially explain the unrest and extremism of today.

14 a) The USA would now recognize Jerusalem as Israel’s capital

The US embassy in Israel was in Tel Aviv from the 1960s until it was moved to Jerusalem in May 2018. Israel won control of Jerusalem during the Six-Day War of 1967 and it remains contested. For both Jews and Muslims the city has deep religious significance. Israel regards Jerusalem as its eternal, indivisible capital, while many Palestinians argue that East Jerusalem should be the capital of any future Palestinian state. The White House suggested that the embassy move did not rule out East Jerusalem becoming the capital of a Palestinian state one day.

24 BP (British Petroleum)

Following the discovery of oil in Persia, the Anglo-Persian Oil Company was formed in 1909, and the British government bought the controlling share in 1914. The British built the world’s biggest refinery at the port of Abadan, and from it flowed cheap oil for the British Empire, until Iran’s government voted to nationalize its oil industry. In 1954 it became the British Petroleum Company.

28 b) About 20% of the world’s total oil consumption moves through it every year

At its narrowest the Strait of Hormuz is just 34 km across. This is Iran’s only way out to the open ocean lanes, but its narrow width means that Tehran can threaten to close it to everyone else. Given that one-fifth of global oil supplies pass through it, closure would cause massive disruption to oil and gas shipments from Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia and the UAE, leading to a huge rise in energy prices and potentially a global recession.

40 Cedar tree

The cedar tree has been linked with Lebanon as far back as the time of King Solomon 3,000 years ago. The Book of Hosea (14:5–6) contains one of the Bible’s numerous references connecting them: ‘I will be like the dew to Israel; he will blossom like the lily; he will take root like the cedars of Lebanon.’ This unifying symbol represents a country that is made up of a patchwork quilt of ethnicities and religions: Sunnis, Shia, Druze, Alawites, Catholics, Maronites and others.
