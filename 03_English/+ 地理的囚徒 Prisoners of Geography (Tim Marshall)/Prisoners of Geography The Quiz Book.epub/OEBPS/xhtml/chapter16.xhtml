5 c) 1900

On 5 July 1900 the British Parliament passed the Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act, which was signed by Queen Victoria four days later; and on 1 January 1901 the six British colonies united to form the Commonwealth of Australia. Half a million people lined the streets of Sydney to celebrate. Australia had not become a sovereign state, only a ‘self-governing colony’ – it wasn’t until 1986 that full independence was declared via the Australia Act – but a great leap forward in self-determination had been achieved.

12 The Murray–Darling River Basin

The two biggest rivers in Australia with permanent flow are the Murray River and its tributary, the Darling River. Fed by melting snows in the Australian Alps, the Murray has enough volume to run uninterrupted for 2,500 km to the southern coast. The basin contains the fertile lands that have fed and watered generations of Australians. Without it the first settlers would barely have got off the beach.

13 b) A third

Australia has abundant natural resources including many that are perfectly suited for selling around the world. It also has the largest zinc and lead deposits, and is a major producer of tungsten and gold, as well as having healthy deposits of silver. It is a key supplier of liquefied natural gas while also still producing large quantities of coal, and its wool, lamb, beef, wheat and wine industries are renowned around the world.

14 c) 4,300 km

Australia is a very large country. Comprising around 7.6 million km2, it is the sixth largest in the world. A similar distance to driving from Brisbane to Perth would be from London to Beirut via France, Belgium, Germany, Austria, Hungary, Serbia, Bulgaria, Turkey and Syria.

15 a) China

China is by far its biggest trading partner. China is the main destination for Australia’s exported farm produce, including products such as beef and barley. It is also a major market for Australia’s iron ore, gas, coal and gold. But China’s wider interests in the region, its attempts to expand its territorial claims and influence, don’t always align with Australia’s. When it comes to China, Australia must walk a difficult line between economic interests, defence strategy and diplomacy.

27 They make Australia vulnerable to blockade

Most of the country’s imports and exports flow through a series of narrow passageways to the north, many of which could be closed in times of conflict. They include the Malacca, Sunda and Lombok Straits. The Malacca Strait is the shortest route from the Indian Ocean to the Pacific. Just this one passageway sees roughly 80,000 vessels pass through it each year, carrying about a third of the world’s traded goods, including around 80% of the oil heading for northeast Asia. In the event of a successful blockade Australia would quickly be in a state of energy crisis. It holds about two months’ oil supply in its strategic reserves onshore.

It is probably the world’s most efficient intelligence-gathering network. There’s nothing quite like this elsewhere in terms of its scope and abilities, and it gives each member unparalleled access to information to guide decision-making.

38 b) The ‘Quad’ (Quadrilateral Security Dialogue)

The Quad is not quite an alliance; it’s more a strategic framework for the navies of the four countries to collaborate in the Pacific. It is not openly stated, but the rationale is to work together to ensure the sea lanes remain open and to curb Chinese influence. There’s been talk of extending to a ‘Quad Plus’, drawing in New Zealand, South Korea and Vietnam, although the latter two are treading carefully due to their proximity to China.
