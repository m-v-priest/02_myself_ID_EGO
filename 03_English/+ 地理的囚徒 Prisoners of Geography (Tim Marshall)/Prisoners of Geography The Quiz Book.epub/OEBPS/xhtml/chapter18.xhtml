9. SPACE

1 V-2 rocket

In 1942 the German scientist Wernher von Braun oversaw the launch of a rocket that could reach sub-orbital space, about 100 km up, but his team could not yet engineer a rocket that was able to achieve the speed required to enter into orbit. However, his Vergeltungswaffe 2 (Vengeance Weapon 2), or V-2 rocket, had a top speed of around 5,300 km/h, and could travel about 320 km before falling back to Earth.

2 Outer Space Treaty

Most of the rules governing the use of space are based on the Outer Space Treaty (1967) but space laws are horribly out of date and too vague for current conditions and our more advanced technology. Most were primarily products of the Cold War, negotiated by the main players. They are no longer fit for purpose.

7 The Cold War

The Space Race was a major battle in the long decades of the Cold War. It became an ideological race to prove which system, capitalism or communism, was technologically superior and could deal the other a psychological blow.

8 cradle

Konstantin Tsiolkovsky was responsible for the first theoretical proof for the possibility of spaceflight, in his 1903 paper ‘Exploration of the World Space with Reaction Machines’, which later propelled him to fame in Russia. His formula, known as ‘the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation’, set out the relationship between the speed of the rocket, the changing mass of the rocket and its fuel, and the speed of the gas as it is expelled. It is the foundation of space travel. His early writings included many visionary ideas: space stations powered by solar energy, airlocks to enable spaceships to dock with one another, pressurized space suits and the concept of a space elevator.

9 b) Satellites or spacecraft can ‘hover’ there to remain in position while using minimal fuel

These points can be seen as ‘car parks’ in space. The gravitational effect means that a third, smaller body (such as a satellite or spacecraft) can sit at the sweet spot. In the future you could deliver a consignment of raw materials mined from an asteroid, or equipment needed to build a space station, to one of these points and be confident that it would still be there when you returned.

10 The International Space Station

In 1993 a deal was agreed between the American, Russian, European, Japanese and Canadian space agencies to build a space station bridging political and cultural divides. In 1998 the Russians took the first piece up and two years later there was enough room for occupants to move in. Since then, more than 160 Americans and over 50 Russians have shared the living quarters and science labs with dozens of other astronauts from all over the world. The ISS is a symbol of what can be achieved in space through cooperation. Sadly, it has almost reached the end of its lifespan and is due to be decommissioned in 2031.

11 Star Wars, announced by Ronald Reagan in 1983

The Strategic Defense Initiative proposed a network of missiles and lasers in space and on the ground. It was never built, due to numerous technical difficulties in developing the lasers and to political opposition on the grounds that it would cause an arms race with the Soviet Union. However, some of the work done on the technology paved the way for current missile defence technology.

14 Because that’s where Earth’s rotation is fastest (about 1,670 km/h)

The most functional location for launch is one that takes maximum advantage of Earth’s rotational speed for the quickest entry into space – thus using less fuel. And so the USA uses the Kennedy Space Center Launch Complex in Florida, as close to the equator as its borders allow, where the speed is about 1,470 km/h. The EU has used French Guiana in South America, while Russia used Kazakhstan. Our planet rotates west to east, and so rockets are launched eastwards to receive an extra boost from Earth’s rotation speed, saving fuel and time. It’s also important that the drop zone for rocket boosters is over mostly uninhabited areas – hence why many launch sites are positioned on eastern coastlines.

20 b) Water

The South Pole–Aitken Basin is 2,500 km wide and 13 km deep. The Sun barely rises above the horizon and so cannot reach the depths of the craters. These permanently shadowed locations are the coldest places observed in the solar system, with temperatures as low as –203ºC recorded, and they may contain ice.

21 C

In geosynchronous and geostationary orbit, 35,786 km from Earth, the speed of the satellite matches the speed of the rotation of Earth and so is above the same piece of territory all the time. The only real difference between them is that a satellite in geosynchronous orbit can circle the planet at any inclination, while a geostationary satellite always follows the equator. This is where the Americans hold their six dual-use Advanced Extremely High Frequency Satellites that communicate with their war planes, with the British, Dutch, Australian and Canadian militaries, and with the US nuclear early-warning system. Russia and China are thought to have similar satellites in place. There is limited room up here, though, due to signal interference, and most of the world’s satellites reside in low Earth orbit (labelled A), as does the ISS.

26 Solar sails

JAXA packed intricately folded panels into a small rocket that took off from the Uchinoura Space Center on Kyushu Island. It then released two sails, one in a clover-leaf shape 10 metres across, the other like a pleated fan, each many times thinner than a sheet of paper. The Japanese proved that large, ultra-light structures can be folded and released intact. Several countries are now working on prototypes of larger, thinner models made of reflective heat-resistant materials that will act as solar panels and propel spaceships vast distances at incredible speeds.

27 b) Van Allen

The belts contain high-energy particles trapped by Earth’s magnetic field. Concentrations of radiation vary, but in places they are high enough to fry a spacecraft’s electronics and, over time, damage human cells.

28 c) Six months

The Moon is on average 385,000 km from Earth, a mere 1.3 light seconds away – the time it takes light to travel from the Moon to our rock. The quickest journey to date was that of the New Horizons spacecraft – eight hours and thirty-five minutes – but most crewed journeys take about three days.

34 c) Japan

NASA estimates there are more than 27,000 pieces of debris in orbit around Earth that are larger than 10 cm in diameter (roughly the size of a grapefruit). There could be another 500,000 sized between 1 and 10 cm (a tennis ball is about 7 cm) and about 100 million bigger than 1 mm. Most pieces of debris may be small, but they are travelling at 25,000 km/h, which would be troubling if you came into contact with one. A 1-cm fragment travelling at that speed can create as much energy as a small car crashing into you, or your spaceship, at 40 km/h.

35 c) GPS (Global Positioning System)

America’s Global Positioning System (GPS) gave US soldiers better navigational abilities in this desert war. Today, GPS uses a minimum of twenty-four satellites distributed equally around the planet to create permanent coverage. Space Force is now responsible for GPS satellites, which can detect missile launches.
