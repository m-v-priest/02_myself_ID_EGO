3 b) The USA, Canada and Europe

Africa is a much bigger landmass than is suggested by looking at a world map. The map that most of us are familiar with uses something called the ‘Mercator projection’, which is one method for rendering a three-dimensional sphere in two dimensions, but as it depicts a sphere on a flat surface, the shapes become distorted. On a traditional map, it looks as if Africa and Greenland are approximately the same size, when in fact Africa is fourteen times bigger – a massive 30.37 million km2.

7 b) Frequent waterfalls and fast currents make it hard to transport goods for any distance

For example, the mighty Zambezi is Africa’s fourth-longest river, running for 2,500 km through six countries, but as a trade route it is of little use. Along its length there are white-water rapids, the Victoria Falls, and it drops from about 1,500 metres at its source to sea level when it reaches the Indian Ocean in Mozambique. Parts of it are navigable by shallow boats, but these parts do not interconnect, thus limiting the transportation of cargo. This drawback hindered contact and trade between regions – which in turn affected economic development, and hampered the formation of large trading regions.

17 d) More than nine out of ten

It’s estimated that 95% of Egypt’s 114 million people live on the Nile’s banks or floodplain, making those regions some of the most heavily populated areas in the world. The ancient Egyptians called the Nile the Ar or Aur, which meant black, because of the dark sediments it carries when the river floods. It is the flooding that deposits fertile soil on its floodplain.

18 c) It lacked the trees to build a great navy

There has always been an Egyptian navy – the country used to import cedar from Lebanon to build ships at huge expense – but it has never been a blue-water navy.

19 Water

Ethiopia is known as ‘Africa’s water tower’ because of its high elevation and the rivers fed by the rainfall in its highlands. It uses the flow of water to generate hydroelectricity, including a dam built with China on the Blue Nile, known as the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam. It’s used for electricity but, in theory, Ethiopia could reduce or cut off the flow of water into Egypt. This is a source of mounting tension.

24 China

It is estimated that China has lent African nations more than $170 billion. They are now as involved across the continent as the Europeans and Americans, if not more so. Resources including energy and the precious metals to be found in many African countries are important to China, which means the Chinese have arrived and they will stay. As well as operating lithium mines in Zimbabwe, Zambia and Namibia, and other mineral mines across the continent, China has built numerous ports, hydroelectric facilities, more than 12,000 km of railway, 1,000 bridges and 95,000 km of road.

28 The Sahara Desert

Once a lush savannah, somewhere between 5,000 and 10,000 years ago the land turned to desert and proved a difficult barrier to cross for centuries. Almost the entire continent below the Sahara developed in isolation from the Eurasian landmass. That is less the case now, but the legacy remains.

30 c) Monrovia

Monrovia is the capital, largest city and chief Atlantic port of Liberia. It was founded on 25 April 1822, during James Monroe’s presidency, by the American Colonization Society as a settlement for freed American slaves. On 26 July 1847, Liberia declared its independence from the Society and was eventually recognized by the USA in 1862.

33 East African Rift

Astronauts say the African Rift is the most significant physical detail they can see from space. Its valleys average about 50 km in width. They begin in Syria and stretch southwards down to Mozambique, 6,000 km away.
