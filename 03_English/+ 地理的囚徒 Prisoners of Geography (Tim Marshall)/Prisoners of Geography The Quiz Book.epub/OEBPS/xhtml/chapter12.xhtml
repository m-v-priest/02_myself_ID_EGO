31 a) It’s the narrowest point of the North European Plain

The North European Plain encompasses all of western and northern France, Belgium, the Netherlands, northern Germany, Denmark and nearly all of Poland. In Poland the plain is only around 500 km wide, running from the Baltic Sea in the north to the Carpathian Mountains in the south, a relatively narrow corridor into which Russia could drive its armed forces if necessary and thus prevent an enemy from advancing towards Moscow. But from this point the plain begins to broaden; by the time you get to Russia’s borders it is over 3,000 km wide, and is flat all the way to Moscow and beyond. Even with a large army, Russia would be hard pressed to defend in strength along this line.

39 Maginot Line

In this code, the vowels have been removed. The Maginot Line was a series of fortifications built before the Second World War, intended to protect France’s border from German invasion, although ultimately the German forces circumvented it.

40 b) GIUK

GIUK stands for Greenland, Iceland, UK. This choke point in the world’s sea lanes has traditionally given the UK an advantage in the north Atlantic. The alternative route for north European navies (including those of Belgium, the Netherlands and France) to access the Atlantic is through the English Channel, which is narrow – only 30 km across at the Strait of Dover – and very well defended. Any Russian naval ship coming from the Arctic also has to pass through the GIUK on its way to the Atlantic. This strategic advantage has diminished in tandem with the reduced role and power of the Royal Navy, but in time of war it would again benefit the UK.
